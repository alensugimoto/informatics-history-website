Google
The 2000s were the first decade ever where consumers really related to web search. For the first time this powerful medium was open to anyone and if we wanted to summarize this revolution with one word this would simply be "Google" In a similar way, considering internet research as a religion, in the 1990s it was polytheistic. In fact, there were various search engines such as Infoseek, Excite, Lycos and others. Among these the most performing was definitely Yahoo, but we cannot say it had the supremacy over the others.
In the 2000s, web search became monotheistic, web search became Google, so much so that new specific terms were coined, such as "I googled this".

Google has played, and is still playing, a crucial role in shaping the modern internet as we understand it today. The way we live our lives has been conditioned by the powerful medium of the internet made more accessible mainly thanks to google.

In 1996 Larry Page and Sergey Brin, two of Stanford University's greatest minds, developed in collaboration with Scott Hassan and Alan Steremberg what we know today as the "PageRank" (name deriving from Larry Page) the revolution that led to Google.
PageRank is an analysis algorithm that assigns a numerical weight to each element of a set of documents connected by means of hyperlinks, used across all pages in the World Wide Web, with the aim of quantifying the relative importance of pages and return results before others. The complete algorithm for calculating PageRank makes use of the Markov process theory and is classified in the true category of Link Analysis Ranking algorithms.

Google.com was then registered in 1997 and made public in 1998. The company, like many others in history (see Apple or Microsoft), started its business in a garage, more precisely in Susan Wojcicki's in Menlo Park, California.
